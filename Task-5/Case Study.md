## 📋 Case Study — UPI Payment Fraud (200–250 words)

### Background

UPI (Unified Payments Interface) fraud is one of the fastest-growing forms of cybercrime in India. Unlike traditional bank fraud, UPI scams exploit social engineering — the victim is manipulated into authorising the transaction themselves, making it legally complex to reverse.

### The Incident

In a widely reported case type across India, a college student received a WhatsApp message from an unknown number claiming to be from their bank's customer support. The message stated that their UPI account would be suspended unless they verified their details immediately. The student was sent a link to a fake website that looked identical to their bank's official page.

After entering their UPI PIN on the fake site, ₹23,000 was transferred out of their account in three transactions within minutes. By the time the student contacted their real bank, two transactions had already been settled.

### What Went Wrong

The victim made three critical errors: they trusted an unverified contact, clicked a link from an unknown source, and entered their UPI PIN on a website they did not independently verify. The scammer used urgency ("your account will be suspended") as a psychological trigger to bypass rational thinking.

### Outcome

A complaint was filed at cybercrime.gov.in and on helpline **1930**. The bank froze one pending transaction, recovering ₹8,000. The remaining amount was not recovered.

## 📝 Reflection 

Writing this case study made me look at UPI fraud differently. It is not a "tech" crime — it is a psychological one. The scammer did not hack anything. They used fear and urgency to make a normal person bypass their own common sense. That is far more dangerous than a technical attack, because it can happen to anyone regardless of how tech-savvy they are.

The most important thing I learned is that **no legitimate bank or payment service ever asks for your UPI PIN** — not by call, not by message, not by link. If someone is asking for it, the conversation should end immediately. Knowing where to report — cybercrime.gov.in and helpline 1930 — is equally critical, because acting fast is the only way to have a chance at recovering funds.
